## **Background**

### **General Information**

This project audits a baseline random forest model that predicts football player transfer fees using historical transfer records from 2000-2018 alongside FIFA video game attributes. We extend the original analysis by rigorously evaluating the model for geographic and systemic biases, specifically applying fairness metrics across demographic axes like player nationality and current league. By quantifying the tension between raw predictive precision and equitable valuation, the audit highlights areas of potential disparate impact within the algorithm and ultimately, this framework provides actionable insights for developing more objective, transparent, and fair player assessment tools in sports analytics.

### **Purpose and Goals**

The purpose of the original solution is to be able to predict transfer fees from attributes of players pulled from FIFA. The purpose of the ADS is to better account for bias and produce a solution that is more interpretable on a sub level. For instance, we want to see if demographic features such as country of birth or the league they are being transferred to have a strong influence on transfer fees and produce a result that is more interpretable for a user. We have also seen a shift in the perspective of using data in football, with more and more football clubs embracing bigger data teams. As a result, we think that models like this will be used to determine player prices in the future. To prevent the same cycle of dominance and the same clubs remaining in poverty, we want to ensure fairness among players and check that players of different demographics are treated fairly. This ADS directly connects to course themes of proxy discrimination, fairness metrics, and the tradeoff between model accuracy and equitable outcomes

## **Input and Output**

### **Dataset Description**

#### **Transfer Fee Dataset**

This dataset contains transfer fees for players between the years 2000 and 2018\. Specifically, the dataset only covers the top 250 transfers per season, thus not all the transfers made in this period were recorded. The dataset is from Kaggle and has 4700 total rows and 10 columns in this dataset. The source of this dataset isn’t specified anywhere in the description on Kaggle, however it likely came from Transfermarkt.

#### **FIFA Dataset**

This is a series of datasets from the video game FIFA. The dataset contains players, their overall ratings, and specific attribute analytics for each player (how fast the game rates them, their shooting rating, passing rating, etc.). There is some bias in the dataset as the game rates players themselves and not from objective measures, but for the most part, FIFA has done a good job rating players accurately. The biggest issue to consider in this dataset is that only FIFA 15-18 were used in the original solution so it is difficult to incorporate FIFA attributes into our ADS if we want our solution to generalize prior to 2015 (unless we pull from other sources). The source of this dataset was from sofifa (publicly available website displaying FIFA stats) and the owner of the dataset scraped off this website. The dataset has 104 columns and 15465 players, however we won’t use all columns and rows as not all players were transferred, and not all columns are relevant for our ADS. We also want to see if it is possible to scrape and incorporate wage data, both raw and done as a percentile of the squad. We know that wages are a good indicator of a squad’s wealth, meaning they are less willing to sell players due to their financial well standing. It is also an indicator that shows a player’s importance, if they are earning a higher wage than the rest of the squad.

### **Feature Description**

#### **Transfer Fee Dataset**

After cleaning (dropping the three rows that report `Position == "Midfielder"` or `"Defender"` rather than a more specific role, and the single age-zero record for Marzouq Al-Otaibi), we are left with 4,696 transfers across 19 seasons (2000–2018), with 247–250 transfers per season. The 10 columns split into:

- Identifiers and categorical (7): `Name`, `Position` (12 distinct roles after cleanup), `Team_from` (570 clubs), `League_from` (118 leagues), `Team_to` (324 clubs), `League_to` (64 leagues), and `Season_transferred` (int year). None of these are missing.
- Numerical (3): `Age` (range 15 to 35, mean 24.3, sd 3.2), `Transfer_fee_in_mln` (range 0.82 to 222.0 M €, mean 9.45, median 6.5, with a heavy right tail dominated by Neymar, Mbappé and Coutinho), and `Market_value_in_mln` (range 0.05 to 120 M €, mean 8.6, missing for 1,256 of 4,696 rows or 26.7%, almost entirely pre-2007 transfers when Transfermarkt valuations were not yet published).

[Figure 1: transfer-fee distribution by season (cell 14 of the original notebook) and the Pearson correlation between `Market_value_in_mln` and `Transfer_fee_in_mln` (≈ 0.83 on the 2015–2018 subset; cell 28–29 of the EDA notebook).]

The `Market_value` missingness drives the audit's choice to focus on the FIFA-merged 2015–2018 window. In that window market value is observed for over 95% of rows, and dropping it would erase the single most predictive feature (see SHAP results in §4.3).

#### **FIFA Dataset**

We use the four "complete player" FIFA tables for 2015 through 2018 (15,465 / 14,881 / 17,009 / 17,592 rows; 105 identical columns each). Of those 105 fields, the audited ADS only ingests five: `short_name → Lastname` (used as the join key), `club` (used as the join key), `nationality` (used only for the audit, not as a model feature), `overall` (range 47 to 94, mean ≈ 66, no missingness), and `potential` (range 49 to 95, mean ≈ 71, no missingness). Wage, value, position, height, weight, and the 100+ skill ratings are dropped before modelling. The original Kaggle author argued the engineered `overall` already summarises the skill columns, so adding them was redundant.

The full 64,947-row concatenation is profiled in `notebooks/eda_transfers_ratings.ipynb` (cells 16–22), where pairwise Spearman correlations show `overall ↔ value_eur` ≈ 0.86 and `overall ↔ wage_eur` ≈ 0.83. In other words, the FIFA "overall" is itself a tight proxy for the in-game money the dataset assigns each player. FIFA ratings are produced by EA scouts and are themselves a known source of bias: the SofiFA dataset over-represents European leagues and under-rates South American and African players relative to their real-world transfer outcomes. We treat this as a pre-existing data bias rather than something the model can fix, and return to it in §5.

[Figure 2: Spearman correlation of `overall` and `Transfer_fee_in_mln` by destination `League_to` (top-10 leagues), reproduced from `eda_transfers_ratings.ipynb` cell 43. The chart reveals that the relationship between player ability and transfer fee varies significantly by destination league. Premier League and LaLiga show ρ ≈ 0.55 to 0.65, while Russian and Chinese Super League show ρ near zero or negative, motivating the league-aware fairness analysis in §4.2.]

[Figure 3: Spearman correlation between FIFA `overall` and transfer fee broken down by `Nationality` (cell 44 of the EDA notebook). For Scotland, Argentina and Senegal the correlation is negative, which means that for these nationalities a higher FIFA rating does not translate into a higher fee. This is already a hint that nationality is a fairness-relevant axis even before any model is trained.]

### **System Output**

The system is a `RandomForestRegressor` (sklearn) that emits a single continuous prediction in millions of euros for each candidate transfer. There is no probability, no class label, and no calibrated uncertainty in the original solution. The original Kaggle author validates only against Mean Squared Error and R² on a single 30% held-out split. Because the target is right-skewed (median 6.5 M €, max 222 M €), squared-error metrics are dominated by a small number of mega-transfers, which is one of the audit motivations for adding MAE, signed mean residual, calibration by predicted-fee bin, and 90% conformal intervals in §4.

Since the original solution did not incorporate fairness metrics at all, our audit adds a COMPAS-style suite of per-group signed residual (who is systematically over- or under-predicted), per-group MAE and RMSE (who absorbs the largest mistakes), per-bin calibration (do residuals differ at comparable predicted fees), and a counterfactual fairness check (does the same player rated identically by FIFA receive a different prediction if their nationality is swapped to W. Europe?). We treat `Nationality` and `Region` as the primary protected axes and `League_from` as a proxy or contextual axis. Standard demographic-parity ratios are not reported because the target is continuous; we use signed-residual gap and MAE gap as the regression analogues.

## **Implementation and Validation**

### **Data Preprocessing**

Data cleaning standardised the 17 raw position labels down to 12 (`Centre-Forward` and `Second Striker` mapped to `Forward`, `Sweeper` mapped to `Defender`), normalised transfer fees into millions of euros, and removed three obvious data-quality outliers (the age-zero record and two records mis-labelled as `Midfielder` or `Defender`). Together these drops are less than 0.1% of the data. The pipeline integrated the FIFA ratings by fuzzy-matching team names (`fuzzywuzzy` with a similarity threshold of 80) and performing a two-stage left merge keyed on `(Lastname, Season, Club)`: first against the player's origin club, then a fallback against the destination club for transfers that did not match. A final broader Lastname+Season fallback recovers an additional handful of records.

Two preprocessing choices shape the rest of the audit. The first is that our cleaned pipeline (`notebooks/cleaned_model_pipeline.ipynb`, cell 15) deduplicates the FIFA side by keeping only the highest-`overall` record per `(Lastname, Club, Season)` triple. The original Kaggle author kept all matching FIFA rows, which means a single transfer could match multiple FIFA records when the same lastname appeared at two clubs in the same season; this inflated his sample to 731 rows. After deduplication the audit-grade sample is 382 transfers across 2015–2018 with FIFA `overall` and `potential` joined in. The second is that the audited model keeps `Name`, `Lastname`, `Team_from`, `Team_to`, and `Market_value_in_mln` exactly as the original Kaggle author did (so that §4 audits the model the author actually deployed), while the engineered fairness columns (`Nationality`, `Region`, `league_median_fee_to`, season-normalised fee, age and position buckets) are stripped from `X` and held aside as audit context. This mirrors how the original ADS would behave in production while giving us the side-channel we need for §4.

### **High Level Implementation of System**

The implementation begins by integrating historical transfer records from 2000–2018 with detailed player performance attributes from FIFA video-game datasets (2015–2018) to build a feature set for the 382-row 2015–2018 modelling window. After establishing two weak baselines (a Linear Regression on the same FIFA-enriched features at R² = 0.17, MSE = 101.75, and a Random Forest trained on the full 4,696-row 2000–2018 transfer table without any FIFA features at R² = 0.36, MSE = 60.72), the audited ADS itself is a `RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=270, max_features=650, random_state=169)` fit on the FIFA-enriched 267-row training split with 948 one-hot-encoded features. Those features are `Position`, `Team_from`, `Team_to`, `Age`, `Market_value_in_mln`, `Season_transferred`, FIFA `overall`, FIFA `potential`, plus dummies for player `Name` and `Lastname` that the original author also kept. Sample-weighted, hierarchical and conformal variants are added in §§ 9–13 of the notebook for the audit but are not part of the deployed ADS.

The original Kaggle author concluded that while FIFA ratings serve as effective proxies for ability, transfer fees are significantly influenced by market inflation and extreme financial outliers. Our SHAP analysis (§4.3) reinforces this conclusion: `Market_value_in_mln` alone contributes 57.9% of the model's feature importance, with `potential` (15.3%) and `overall` (6.4%) trailing far behind, and the long tail of player-name dummies absorbing the remaining 20%.

### **ADS Validation and Stated Goals**

The original Kaggle author validated the ADS using a single 30% held-out split and reported R² ≈ 0.80, MSE ≈ 48.7, RMSE ≈ 6.92 M € on a 731-row sample (`adjusted_original_notebook.ipynb`, cell 66). When we re-validate the same architecture in our cleaned pipeline (`cleaned_model_pipeline.ipynb`, cell 26) on the deduplicated 382-row sample described above, the model achieves R² = 0.668, RMSE = 6.40 M €, MAE = 4.25 M €, and mean signed bias = +0.05 M € on the 115-row test set. Section 8a of the cleaned notebook then reproduces the original loose merge inside the same notebook for an apples-to-apples comparison and obtains R² = 0.705 on a 685-row sample. The gap between the two numbers is driven almost entirely by the merge strategy. Keeping every `(Lastname, Club, Season)` match rather than collapsing duplicates inflates the training set, and lifts headline R² by 4 to 13 points depending on the random split. We treat R² ≈ 0.67 to 0.80 as the model's honest accuracy band; both numbers are dominated by the same handful of mega-transfers (Neymar, Mbappé, Coutinho) on which the model's residuals are largest.

In terms of stated goals, the original ADS was validated only for global predictive performance. Interpretability, calibration, fairness and uncertainty were never assessed. The remainder of this report adds those checks: per-group accuracy and signed-residual fairness (§4.1 and §4.2), SHAP, LIME, and PDP/ICE interpretability (§4.3), 90% conformal prediction intervals for uncertainty (§4.3), and a counterfactual fairness shadow-model audit (§4.3). Together these answer the question the original validation did not: who pays the price when this model is wrong, and would it be safe to deploy in a real recruitment context?

## **Outcomes**

### **Accuracy**

On the held-out 115-row test set, the audited Random Forest achieves R² = 0.668, RMSE = 6.40 M €, MAE = 4.25 M €, and a near-zero mean signed bias of +0.05 M € (notebook §8). This is a respectable global fit given how much real-world transfer fees vary and how many factors are not in the feature set (real-life match statistics, contract length, agent fees, image-rights deals). However, because R² is computed on only 115 transfers and the target is heavy-tailed, the headline number hides large heterogeneity across subpopulations. We chose MAE alongside RMSE as our primary accuracy metric because MAE is robust to the few mega-transfers that dominate squared error: the median fee in our sample is 6.5 M € but the maximum is 222 M €, so a single missed Neymar prediction can move RMSE by several million while MAE stays interpretable as "the typical mistake the model makes on a typical transfer."

The fairness-evaluation table from notebook §10 (cell 32) breaks the test set down by Region (continental aggregation of `Nationality`) and `League_from` for groups with at least 20 test instances:

| Group axis | Group | n | Mean residual (M €) | Median residual (M €) | MAE (M €) | RMSE (M €) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Region | South America | 20 | +0.46 | +0.06 | 3.88 | 6.55 |
| Region | W. Europe | 59 | −0.89 | +0.28 | 3.71 | 5.58 |
| League_from | LaLiga | 20 | −0.44 | +1.21 | 5.13 | 6.86 |
| League_from | Serie A | 26 | +0.79 | +1.04 | 4.53 | 7.06 |

[Figure 4: boxplot of residuals by Region (cell 34, top-4 regions) showing the distribution under both the as-is RF and the sample-weighted variant.]

Two patterns stand out. First, W. European players are systematically under-predicted by about 0.89 M € on average, while South American players are over-predicted by about 0.46 M €, a signed-residual gap of 1.35 M € between the two largest regional groups. Because the as-is ADS is blind to nationality, this gap is being driven entirely by proxies such as `League_from`, `Team_from`, `Market_value`, and the player-name dummies. Second, the magnitude of error is similar across Regions (MAE 3.71 vs 3.88 M €, gap of only 0.17 M €). The audit's takeaway is not that South-American players get worse predictions, but that they get systematically biased-upward predictions while Western Europeans get systematically biased-downward predictions. For the deployment use-case (a club using the model as a negotiation anchor) this matters in opposite directions: South-American players would be pushed up the buy-list and Western Europeans down, which is the inverse of the equity goal the project set out with.

We also report Nationality-level accuracy in the notebook (cell 32), but no individual nationality has 20 or more test instances, so country-level numbers are dominated by sampling noise and we keep them out of the headline table. Region aggregation is the appropriate granularity for our 115-row test set.

### **Fairness of ADS**

The original ADS does not measure fairness at all. It adds features and lets the random forest discover whatever relationships maximise global RMSE. As the table above shows, this leaves a measurable signed-residual gap of 1.35 M € across Regions and 1.23 M € across `League_from`, even though Region and Nationality are never given to the model as features. The disparity propagates through proxies: `League_from` (clubs in the Premier League and LaLiga act as a high-value tag), `Team_from` and `Team_to` (one-hot dummies for individual clubs), and `Market_value_in_mln` (which is itself a Transfermarkt valuation produced by humans who are subject to the same prestige biases the audit is trying to surface).

To test whether a simple in-processing intervention can close the gap, notebook §9 and §10 train an identical Random Forest with inverse-frequency Region × fee-band sample weights during fitting (`rf_weighted` in cell 32). The intervention shifts the gaps in the wrong direction for fairness on Region (signed gap 1.35 to 1.67 M €, MAE gap 0.17 to 0.25), but it dramatically tightens fairness on `League_from` (MAE gap 0.60 to 0.012). RMSE rises only modestly (6.40 to 6.67 M €). [Figure 5: RMSE-vs-MAE-gap fairness and performance frontier scatter from cell 34.] The intervention is therefore a real Pareto trade-off rather than a free lunch: it buys per-league fairness at the cost of slightly worse Region-level fairness and slightly higher overall error. We discuss the deployment implications in §5.

For the calibration check (residuals within predicted-fee quintiles by Region), the notebook §11 Simpson's-paradox audit confirms that the global Region-level pattern is not reversed once we condition on either `League_from` or predicted-fee bin. W. Europe stays under-predicted in 100% of its `League_from` strata and 60% of its predicted-fee bins, so the headline regional gap is real and not a Simpson artefact (cell 36 output).

We deliberately do not report demographic-parity or equalised-odds ratios. Both are classification-fairness constructs that require a binary "positive prediction"; this is a regression problem with a continuous target, and synthesising a threshold would inject a policy choice that the original ADS does not make. We instead report the regression analogues: signed-residual gap, MAE gap, and calibration by predicted-fee bin.

### **Additional Methods to Audit**

We extend the audit along four directions that the original validation never touched: interpretability, uncertainty, robustness to subgroup mis-specification, and counterfactual fairness.

#### Interpretability: SHAP, LIME, PDP and ICE

SHAP TreeExplainer on a 300-row sample of the test set (notebook §13.1) shows that `Market_value_in_mln` is dominant (mean |SHAP| ≈ 4.2 M €), followed by `potential` and `overall`, with the long tail of player-name dummies (`Name_Neymar`, `Lastname_Neymar`, `Team_to_Paris SG`, `Name_Philippe Coutinho`) absorbing the rest. This is concerning because the player-name and team dummies are pure identity features. The model is partially memorising specific record-breaking transfers, which means it will not generalise to a new player it has never seen at a club it has never seen. [Figure 6: SHAP beeswarm and bar chart from cell 44.] LIME (notebook §13.2) confirms this at the extremes. For the most-expensive predicted transfer the local drivers are `Market_value_in_mln > 16` and `Name_Naby Keïta > 0`; for the cheapest predicted transfer the drivers are `Market_value_in_mln ≤ 6` and a negative contribution from `Name_Steven Naismith ≤ 0`. The model relies on different signals at the two ends of the fee distribution, so a single global feature-importance ranking is misleading. PDP and ICE plots for the top-3 features (notebook §13.3, cell 48) show that the marginal effect of `Market_value_in_mln` is monotonically increasing as expected, but `potential` exhibits a flat plateau between 70 and 82 and a sharp jump above 82. The model only rewards potential past a star-player threshold. [Figure 7: PDP and ICE side-by-side panels from cell 48.]

#### Uncertainty: split conformal prediction

Notebook §13.4 builds 90% conformal intervals on the as-is ADS by holding out 53 calibration rows from the training split, fitting a fresh RF on the remaining 214, and using the 90th-percentile absolute residual to set the half-width. Empirical coverage on the test set is 98.3% (over-cover, well above the 90% target), the median interval width is 49.5 M €, and the conformal radius is 24.7 M €. Per-Region coverage is uniformly 95% to 100%, so the intervals are fair (no group is systematically uncovered), but a ±25 M € band is operationally useless for a club using the model to anchor a negotiation on a 6 M € journeyman transfer. [Figure 8: predicted vs actual with 90% conformal band, cell 50.] This is one of the strongest deployment-relevant findings in the audit: even when the point estimate is good, the honest uncertainty around it spans the entire transfer-fee distribution.

#### Robustness: Simpson's paradox sweep

Notebook §11.1 (cell 38) sweeps six (group × strata) pairs and finds two flagged sign-reversals. `Region == "Other"` and `Region == "South America"` both have global mean residuals that flip sign within every predicted-fee bucket the test sample is large enough to evaluate (`same_sign_share = 0.0`). Both groups have small global n (15 and 20), so the flag warns the reader that any future deployment should refuse to issue per-region pricing guidance until subgroup-level samples grow. [Figure 9: paradox-rate heatmap from cell 38 and sign-consistency bar chart for AgeBucket, PositionBucket and LeagueTier.] The hierarchical model in §12 (cell 40) is the natural mitigation, training one local residual model per region with at least 80 training instances. On this dataset only W. Europe meets the threshold, and the resulting hierarchical predictor worsens RMSE from 6.40 to 9.69 and the Region MAE gap from 0.17 to 0.50, confirming that there is simply not enough non-W.Europe data to support local correction.

#### Counterfactual fairness: Region-aware shadow model

Notebook §15 (cell 52) trains a shadow Random Forest with the same hyperparameters as the as-is ADS plus seven Region one-hot dummies as additional inputs. For every non-W.Europe test player it then computes the prediction under their actual Region and the prediction after swapping that Region to `W. Europe`. The mean geographic premium across the 56 non-W.Europe test players is +0.60 M € (median +0.46, max +2.56), and 80.4% of players receive a positive premium when relabelled as European. [Figure 10: geographic-premium histogram from cell 52.] This is the audit's clearest finding: a Region-aware version of the same architecture would, on average, value the same player at FIFA-identical statistics about 600 K € higher if their nationality were European. The as-is ADS, which never sees Region, already encodes about 0.89 M € of this gap through `League_from`, `Team_from` and `Market_value` proxies. Comparing the §4.2 signed-residual gap to the +0.60 M € counterfactual premium implies that roughly two thirds of the latent geographic premium is already leaking into the blind model through proxies.

## **Summary**

### **Appropriate Data**

The dataset is useful for building a performance baseline by merging objective FIFA attributes with historical transfer records to analyze how technical skill correlates with market price. However, it fails to account for critical economic mediators like league revenue, where the financial power of the Premier League often inflates fees regardless of a player's actual performance rating. This lack of context allows systemic biases to persist, such as the "homegrown" premium which can lead a model to falsely conclude that being English is an inherent marker of higher value. Additionally, the data does not capture geographic pedigree or commercial brand value, meaning players from "traditional" football nations may receive higher valuations based on prestige rather than merit. Ultimately, while appropriate for a technical audit, the data remains insufficient for a truly fair system because it mirrors existing market inequalities rather than identifying more objective, equitable valuations.

### **Implementation robust, accurate and fair**

No on all three counts, in different ways. On accuracy, the headline R² of 0.67 (audit) or 0.80 (original) is reasonable as a global summary but is inflated by `Market_value_in_mln` carrying 58% of the SHAP importance. The model is largely re-predicting Transfermarkt's own valuation rather than discovering an independent signal: the original author's own ablation drops from 80% to 44% R² when `Market_value` is removed. On robustness, the 90% conformal interval is ±25 M € on top of a 6.5 M € median fee, which means the honest uncertainty around any single prediction spans the entire transfer-fee distribution; the Simpson sweep additionally flags two regions where the global residual sign reverses inside every predicted-fee bucket. On fairness, the as-is ADS leaks about 1.35 M € of signed-residual gap across Regions through proxies even though it is blind to Nationality, and our counterfactual shadow model shows that 80% of non-W.Europe players would receive a higher prediction if relabelled as European. That is a direct measurement of latent geographic bias that the ADS would propagate at scale.

We chose MAE and signed-residual gap as the primary fairness measures because they are interpretable in real money to the stakeholders who would actually use the model. A club's analytics director cares whether her recommended bid is systematically 0.9 M € low for European players (so she will overpay relative to the model) and 0.5 M € high for South Americans (so she will underpay). MAE-gap is the right metric for selling clubs (whose revenue depends on who pays the largest mistakes); signed-residual gap is the right metric for agents and players (whose career earnings depend on which direction the bias points); conformal-interval coverage is the right metric for regulators or league offices that might one day audit such a tool.

### **Deploying ADS in public sector**

Not in its current form, in either sector. For the public sector (such as league-level financial-fair-play oversight, or a national federation using the model to evaluate transfer balances), three blockers apply. First, the data is itself biased: the `Market_value` field is a Transfermarkt human valuation with documented prestige effects, and FIFA `overall` is produced by EA scouts who systematically over-rate European leagues, so any "objective" prediction the model produces is downstream of two layers of human bias. Second, the conformal interval of ±25 M € makes it impossible to honestly defend a fine or a sanction grounded in a model output. Third, the counterfactual gap of +0.6 M € for European relabelling means the model would, on aggregate, push regulators to undervalue non-European talent, a direct disparate-impact concern.

For industry deployment (a club's recruitment department), the model is acceptable as one of many anchors a human analyst consults, provided that the prediction is always reported alongside its 90% conformal interval, the user is shown the SHAP attribution (so they can see when `Market_value` is doing all the work), and predictions for non-W.Europe players are flagged for human override. Without those guardrails the model would reproduce and arguably amplify the very same "rich-club, European-pedigree" market dynamics the original Kaggle author was trying to study.

### **Improvements** 

One external source that could be useful would be incorporating real-life match statistics (Opta, StatsBomb, FBref). These would serve as a better proxy for performance than FIFA's editorial ratings and would directly attack the FIFA-side bias documented in §2. A second improvement is to strip the player-name and club one-hot dummies from the feature set: SHAP shows they contribute about 20% of importance through pure identity memorisation, which both inflates headline R² and makes the model brittle on unseen players. A third improvement is to add stratified sampling on `Region × fee-band`. The §9 intervention shows this is the right direction even if our specific weight schedule slightly hurt Region MAE-gap; a more careful schedule should be Pareto-improving. A fourth, mandatory improvement before any deployment, is to publish the model card and dataset nutrition label: the audit numbers in §4.1 to §4.3 are exactly the inputs such a card would require. Finally, the data-collection layer could be made fairer by broadening the transfer source beyond Transfermarkt's "top 250" feed (which itself over-samples high-value moves). Including the long tail of mid-table moves would shrink the dependence on `Market_value` and let the model learn structural features (age, position, league) more honestly.
